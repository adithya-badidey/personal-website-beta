Introduction to Sociological Theory

Since antiquity, there are two schools of thought about what influences human behaviour the most - the "naturalistic" and the "individualistic" explanations.

The naturalistic explanation proposes that all human behaviour is governed our inherited, naturalistic dispositions. The individualistic explanation rests the behaviour of human beings on their individual unique and particular phychological and physiological qualities. Both these explanations fail at explaining certain common traits of human behaviour.

If all behaviour is naturalistic, how is there so much change and deviation from the normal - most of society should be more or less uniform from one generation to another. If everyone were individualistic, they would marry, reproduce, study, buy, work and die in unique ways. This is not so however, since much of human behaviour is observed to be shared in social categories. A person born in the middle class is more likely to study in college than those from the working class.

Humans are social beings. Sociological theories examine the social dimension to human existance - they emphasize the ways human beliefs and actions are a product of social influence. They all try to explain how remarkably consistent human behaviour is - almost everyone we meet on a day-to-day basis behaves in an expected manner. Some basic social theories are the consensus, conflict and action theories.

Structural Consensus Theory

Structural Consensus theory argues that society's cultural rules (which are decided by consensus) determine the structure of thought and behaviour for a person, akin to how a physical building governs the actions of people inside it. Individuals, therefore, behave similarly in social settings because they are bound by their social structure. Read more.

Structural Conflict Theory

Structural Conflict theory argues that society has a lot of unequality and that the behaviour of people is constrained by the advantages or privileges they possess (or dont possess) by nature of their position in these structures of inequality. Read more.

Social Action Theory

According to the Action theory, the most important determinant for an Individual's behaviour is how they percieve their environment and the behaviour of others towards them. Read more.
