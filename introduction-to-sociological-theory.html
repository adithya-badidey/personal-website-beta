Introduction to Sociological Theory

Introduction

Humans are social beings. Since antiquity, there are two schools of thought about what influences human behaviour the most - the "naturalistic" and the "individualistic" explanations.

The naturalistic explanation proposes that all human behaviour is governed our inherited, naturalistic dispositions. The individualistic explanation rests the behaviour of human beings on their individual unique and particular phychological and physiological qualities. Both these explanations fail at explaining certain common traits of human behaviour. If all behaviour is naturalistic, how is there so much change and deviation from the normal - most of society should be more or less uniform from one generation to another. If everyone were individualistic, they would marry, reproduce, study, buy, work and die in unique ways. This is not so however, since much of human behaviour is observed to be shared in social categories. A person born in the middle class is more likely to study in college than those from the working class.

Sociological theories examine the social dimension to human existance - they emphasize the ways human beliefs and actions are a product of social influence. Some basic social theories are the consensus, conflict and action theories.
