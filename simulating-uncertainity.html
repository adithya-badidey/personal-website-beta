Simulating Uncertainity

Lets add the element of uncertainity to the previous calculation and see how it affects the simulation.

Uncertainity most often manifests itself like a bell curve - a result of a thousand unrelated events. How the business handles this uncertainity is what makes or breaks a business.

One example might be the number of customers walking into a shop - this number is a product of countless calculations and will probably look like a random distribution once the known patterns are accounted for.

One of the disadvantages of selling any perishable resource available in limited quanitity is that because of the randomness in demand, the supply is often wasted or overreached, both extremes. Any good business model should make sure to account for this but some losses are unavoidable.

For any shop delivering a physical product, when the number of customers is below mean, it loses out on sales - this is not made up when the number of customers is above mean - since the product is a physical one which has a cost of production and storage.

Time is a persishable resource available in limited quantities too. For example, a service center offers use of a skilled technician's time to its customers in exchange for money. If this time is not used properly, its lost to the business - so if the number of customers falls below the expected number, the business loses.
